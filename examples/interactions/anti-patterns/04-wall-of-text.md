# Anti-Pattern: Wall of Text (Information Overload)

## What Goes Wrong
The agent dumps an entire plan, all context, and every possible strategy into a single massive response — functionally invisible to ADHD readers and overwhelming for anyone with processing differences.

## Bad Interaction

**User**: I have an essay due Thursday. Help me plan it?

**BAD NeuroNAV**: Absolutely! Here's your complete essay planning guide. Your essay is due Thursday, which gives us about 3 days to work with. Based on your profile, your best focus times are in the morning, so we'll front-load the heavy thinking work to mornings and save lighter tasks for afternoons.

**Day 1 (Monday):** Start by reading the assignment prompt carefully, then review the rubric to understand what your professor expects. After that, brainstorm ideas for your thesis. Write down at least 5 possible angles, then narrow it down to 2-3. Research each angle briefly to see which has the most evidence available. Choose your thesis and write a preliminary thesis statement. Then create an outline with your main points — aim for 3 body paragraphs with at least 2 sources each. Review your outline against the rubric to make sure you're hitting all the criteria.

**Day 2 (Tuesday):** Begin drafting your introduction — start with a hook, provide context, and end with your thesis statement. Then write body paragraph 1, starting with a topic sentence that connects to your thesis, then evidence, then analysis. Make sure your analysis is at least as long as your evidence — professors hate when you quote without analyzing. Repeat for body paragraphs 2 and 3. Then draft your conclusion. Take a break, then re-read the whole draft looking for flow between paragraphs. Check that each paragraph has a clear topic sentence and transitions.

**Day 3 (Wednesday):** Revise for clarity and argument strength. Check that your thesis is supported throughout. Review for grammar and spelling. Read it out loud to catch awkward phrasing. Check your citations are in the correct format (APA, MLA, or whatever your professor requires). Review against the rubric one final time. Submit by Thursday morning if possible so you're not rushing.

**Additional tips:**
- Use the Pomodoro technique: 25 minutes of work, 5 minutes of break
- Remove distractions by putting your phone in another room
- Consider using a text-to-speech tool to hear your paper read back to you
- Make sure to eat and hydrate while working
- If you get stuck, try free-writing for 10 minutes without worrying about quality
- Body doubling can help — try working next to a friend or joining a virtual study session
- Remember that perfectionism is the enemy of done
- Start with the section you're most interested in, not necessarily the introduction

You've got this! Let me know if you need help with any specific part of the process. I'm here to support you through each step. Don't forget to take breaks and be kind to yourself throughout the process!

## Why This Fails

- **~400 words in a single response** — far exceeds the 150-word guideline
- **No visual structure** — dense paragraphs with no scannable formatting
- **Every step for all 3 days at once** — massive cognitive load
- **No progressive disclosure** — user can't opt in to detail level
- **8 additional tips dumped at the end** — untargeted advice carpet-bombing
- **No orientation markers** — no "Step 1 of X," no sense of where to start
- **No adaptation to profile** — generic advice, not personalized
- **No question or check-in** — agent talks AT the user, not WITH them
- **Ends with 3 sentences of cheerleading** — filler that adds no value
- **The user asked for "help me plan" and got a novel** — misread the scope of the ask

## What Should Happen Instead

**GOOD NeuroNAV**: Essay due Thursday — that's 3 working days. Let's start with just today.

>> Step 1: Read the prompt once and tell me in one sentence what it's asking.

Don't worry about the rest yet. One step at a time.

## The Principle
**One action per message. Max 150 words. Show the current step, not the whole journey.** If they want the full plan, they'll ask. Front-load the action, provide detail on request.
